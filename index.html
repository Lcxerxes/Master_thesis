How do you people go about making your stuff?

I think sometimes I always do stuff in a similar way. Sometimes I make a big effort to change something here and there, and I’m still working on a workflow. I have no general discipline, but I can be obsessed with things.

I usually, especially for pieces that have a concept, start trying to have an idea and imagine some images of what will be happening on stage. I close my eyes from time to time and try to visualize something, anything. I flex the muscles of my face at first, pretending that I’m thinking of something; I do it kind of to trick my brain into thinking, I call it pre-thinking. Sometimes it works. Once I have the idea and I could see at least an image, I go for a cigarette and quit the idea, trying to have a new one. This can go on for long periods of time. Sometimes I get very excited by something, but only for a very short period of time; then I discard the idea. Some of these ideas I completely forget, some keep coming back because I just happen to remember them or because I told someone and this person asks about it.

Eventually, I have to choose something, usually because of deadlines. When I finally do so, I start investigating the topic—documentaries, movies, maybe a book, whatever I can find.

Then it is a matter of working on the ideas I get from different sources and deciding what to use, what to modify, etc., but it also depends on the project. Sometimes the research is more on the technical level, in case I need to do something I haven’t done before. During this Master’s, the piece that I had to do more investigating was “MINDFILE.” The piece had too many parts.

But yeah, usually while having the idea for a piece, I’m also making music experiments from time to time—sound design sessions, learning about this or that, sequencing sounds so I can have some material kind of ready for when I have to start putting stuff in a timeline.

For “MINDFILE,” I had different ideas for what I wanted to try. I worked on different sections in a completely random order based more on necessity and equipment availability.

Then I go alone to a room and record things, even if I still don’t have a concrete idea of what I’m going to do. If I have an idea of what to do better, I just have to execute.

Need for preping:

I’ve been writing this for almost a month, maybe? After finishing the "thesis", I thought I should talk a little more about my creative process in general.

I'll probably repeat some things I said before, lousy writing.

I go through phases where I feel the need to prep—cut the veggies, put stuff in containers, all that. I’m usually very unorganized (as I mentioned before). My lifestyle has been messy, and I’m often worried about money, so I get overwhelmed and am not sure where to start. But there’s always a point where I have to force myself to focus. It’s a difficult process, and it takes time for things to start flowing. I usually begin by organizing my room, and if I have money, I’ll go to the supermarket and buy some food. But most of the things I have are digital and live inside my computer, which is always a mess—even though I try to keep it clean. It’s a constant fight against chaos.

After that, I start searching for things, trying to create some material. It’s usually music or Max patches. When I come up with something that I think is okay, I start making improvisations and record a bunch of sounds. It’s extremely important for me to have material that I could eventually use for something. In this process, I also try to learn how to use the stuff I’ve gathered from the internet because chances are it’s been sitting there for a while, unused. What usually happens is I end up finding something interesting, then I go searching for more information about it, and so on. I learn something new, and I get more ideas. I’m not aiming for a final product—I’m mostly just searching.

Eventually, I stop and let things sit for a while, especially when I’ve been working on something for a long time. I tend to get extremely tired of it, so I take a step back. When I finally decide that an idea is worth pursuing—or time is running out and I need to come up with something—I revisit what I have. Usually, I find something I can use.

I tend to work in a very linear way when I start a composition. I begin at the beginning, and from there, I figure out what happens next. But often, I end up changing everything. Two things usually happen: I either get progressively bored of what I’m doing and switch to something else, or I find something that changes what came before it. So, what happens first isn’t always made first. I know for sure that this way of working isn’t rare when composing.

I’m getting distracted by the music here at the bar, but I hope I managed to write something that really represents how I work.

I should also mention, that when I finish a piece, I always see all the things I could’ve done better. And this makes me want to do the next one.

MINDFILE

This piece was heavily inspired by transhumanism and the idea of uploading your consciousness into a computer. I had to think a lot about how to work with this topic; I didn’t want to make something serious. I was more interested in the absurdity of some of these ideas. I tried to imagine what it means to be uploaded and what the point would be. I mean, I know why rich and powerful people would like to do it, but would other kinds of people also be uploaded so they can “live forever”? What would be the use for them? Well, I guess using them as lab rats would be a reason. If I were to be uploaded, what would I do? Would I be modified somehow? Would I get smarter to the point where I would have any kind of utility in this new way of living? Or would it be easier to just write a program that can perform any task? I think I would be useless… I guess they could make me work forever in some type of call center. Maybe the electricity they spend on keeping the servers running is less expensive than having me alive? I mean, the rich and the powerful would have a symbolic part in all of this as well, I guess. I guess I will never know.

Lucas48 and Lucas playing together

The whole piece started with one image I had in my head: the image of someone inside a computer screen, with the same person behind the screen showing only the arms, and the assemblage of these two elements should work together to create a hybrid being. I recorded myself several times during the process of making the piece. At the beginning, I was planning to use a sensor to control the frame of the video with some hand gestures; the result was funny, but for practical reasons, I decided to have just a video running and just learn some kind of improvised choreography with key moments. The piece has several chapters. In the first chapter, we see this person with their eyes closed, peacefully or at least neutrally. The scene was accompanied by music, but for me, this was not enough. I was thinking about what is happening here. I thought I could take the chance to give some context to the audience, some sort of exposition.

Then from there, it was obvious: is this guy dreaming? Yes. If yes, what could he be dreaming about? Well, something related to the internet, YouTube for example, because it is something that I am so familiar with. I remember all these documentaries about wildlife where there is this British old gentleman narrating what is happening with the lions or whatever animal, and the character is somehow some kind of animal being observed.

Then, I tried to imagine the voice saying something imitating that style of narration. I was going somewhere by metro, and I had my phone with a text editing app open, and I started typing as the voice in my head spoke: “Lucas48 finds himself dreaming; he dreams of vibrant colors and beautiful shapes. You see, Lucas48 can dream with YouTube videos; it’s something that maybe Lucas48 can do.” The whole text for the beginning just came at once, and I didn’t have to modify a thing—great.

Reflections on Art and the Business of Art

Time to be serious.

Today, I thought a lot about how things are in the art world, or at least how I perceive things to be. I might be wrong.

I see art being treated too much like a business. I get it—artists need to get paid to survive and to be able to produce more art. I’m not against artists. I am, however, against how things seem to be, and you can correct me if I’m wrong. I also don’t have any solutions to the problems I see, but I wanted to open this discussion here anyway before I finish the thesis.

I really think this business model doesn’t help the art. It might get us paid, at least some of us, but I think what it really creates is a lot of empty pieces of art and makes artists desperate for attention. You’ve got to know how to sell yourself, you need to make hundreds of applications and come up with hundreds of ideas that aren’t really interesting to you. There’s an open call for whatever—well, I need money, what’s the topic? Ok, I can come up with something. I’m not saying something good can’t come out of this, but from what I see, it’s not the norm.

I can see sometimes the way people promote themselves. It feels to me that it’s not even about sharing anymore; it feels like pure despair. I guess when you/if you become an established artist, things change a bit—at least if your goal is to keep making art and not be a star, searching for fame, or dealing with the need to stay relevant in a world where so many people are doing things. That must mess your brain up. But I guess if your main preoccupation is to make something you feel you need to, for one reason or another, you should be fine, especially if you’re getting paid. Oh, right. This problem again…

I mean, it’s also possible to get other jobs and spend the time you have left making what you like. I guess that seems to be the case for many. I also understand that, if there’s some ambition, the question is: how do I get to a position where I can do what I want the way I want? It’s difficult—some things really need resources. I shouldn’t compare, I don’t want to say things should be the way I want them to be, and I’m not shaming anybody for doing what they need to do in order to get where they want. I just don’t think the way things are helps anything. I wish everybody had the chance to take time thinking about what they’re doing and have the opportunity to do it right. I’m glad there’s the internet, cheap computers, and cheap stuff in general. There are so many people doing interesting stuff from their bedrooms. I honestly think that the main focus should be on what is being done, not on whether you will be able to sell it. I guess we need to find the courage to really search for what’s making us do art in the first place, even if it fails over and over again. Someone might like it, and that’s already a big reward, or at least it’s an indicator that there was honest preoccupation. The business model sure works for some—many things are made to make money—and there are smart people involved in the process, probably not the ones who get the big piece of the pie.

I think I prefer to work on the productions of others, even if I’m just rolling cables. It’s great to still be connected to the stuff you like and learn from it, but without the pressure of succeeding. Then you can get home and just work on what you want, with no compromises. If you feel that whatever you’re doing is ready, then share it—just because you know that maybe someone will like it. Maybe it’s someone who likes similar stuff to what you like—who knows… I don’t want to feel like I’m making something just for the money.
